# Context

A note on the conditions of this run, for readers who want the constraints alongside the results. [lessons_learned.md](./lessons_learned.md) covers what I'd change.

## Conditions

- **Finals overlap.** Rounds 1–2 ran during my Northwestern finals (April 14–20). I made the R1 manual call quickly because of the calendar, not because the problem was easy.
- **Solo.** My intended teammate withdrew before round 1 opened. Every shipped algorithm from R1 onward is solo work. Visible artifacts include the 100+ versions of `combined_ship_v*.py` in round 3 — a second pair of eyes would have culled that branch space faster.
- **Tooling built in-flight.** The calibration harness, jmerle-style backtester wrapper, and ablation scripts were built during the competition rather than ahead of it. Useful in hindsight, expensive in real time.
- **Research-vs-trader gap.** My background is research-side quant work — signal mining, statistical reasoning, prior calibration — and that half held up. What I had less of was trader-side execution discipline: when to stop tuning and ship, how to size against contest mechanics rather than the math, when a structural answer outranks a backtest delta. Closing that gap is the explicit goal of the next contest and of how I'm prepping for the Short Cliffs role.
- **First trading competition.** Prosperity 4 was my first algorithmic-trading contest. The field includes teams with multiple prior Prosperities on the bench. I expect the gap to compress with reps.


## What this entry is

A documented record of a real solo run at an 18 k-team algorithmic-trading competition under finals-week constraints. The strategies are honest, the backtest numbers are real, and the mistakes are catalogued.

One framing I want to be explicit about upfront, since the rest of the writeup is read more cleanly with it in mind: **my approach to the contest was flawed from round 1, and the gap to a top finish is on me, not on the contest design.** I went in optimizing for the shape of trading I'd built up at Hilbert — wider spreads, conservative inventory, structural carry, a real-market fee structure — and treated Prosperity as a faster version of the same problem. It isn't. Prosperity scores you on one realized path against a curated seed under zero fees and ranked-not-returns scoring, which is a different objective. Late rounds rewarded tight-quote market making more cleanly than early rounds did, and my arb-style sleeves had less room to generate edge under those conditions. By round 3 the right read was "the regime has moved against my strategy class — pivot." I read it as round-to-round noise and didn't pivot. The lessons learned are framed around that gap.

The point of the writeup is the reasoning, not the placement. The substance is in [lessons_learned.md](./lessons_learned.md) and the per-round generative-process reads.

## What held up

- **Round 3 manual — data-backed priors and simulation.** The Bio-Pod recommendation [`(775, 875)`](./code/analysis/round3/manual/RECOMMENDATION.md) came out of a six-scenario prior sweep (`avg_b2 ∈ {858, 855, 862, 875, 885, bimodal}`), an integer-grid optimizer over the auction mechanism, and an AI-cluster-aware adjustment for the 55% of teams who paste an LLM answer at the textbook optimum. The reasoning explicitly beat the standalone-EV answer of `(751, 836)`. On top of that I submitted bids [`(771, 861)`] to capture the cluster of teams who had analyzed the problem with the same Bayesian logic I had. That finished world #7 on the challenge, and it's the piece of the competition I'm proudest of: two steps past the obvious answer, derived from first principles plus a field model, with the sensitivity analysis to back it and the market and psychological intuition to capture the edge.
- **Applying past quant internship knowledge directly, not theoretically.** The R3 IV-residual MR sleeve isn't a clever idea; it's a port of the standard ATM-options statistical-arb pattern I'd already implemented on real instruments at Hilbert. The R2 micro-price vs touch-mid spread-regime split is the same idea applied to MM fair-value. The R5 imbalance gate on passive quotes is the same idea applied to adverse selection. The contest rewarded recognizing which standard pattern fit which generative-process story, and I did that consistently round to round. IMC admins probably didn't build this contest as an arbitrage-trading test (the Market Maker piece looks more like the actual talent search), but I stuck to what I knew I was strong at, and I'm proud of the process, results, and learning curve. Excited to learn more about market making over the next few years.
- **Round 5 market analysis.** Reading 50 product news items and producing a coherent per-product side-bias dict in one sitting, with cross-checks against P3 news-trading archetypes for the manual challenge, is a real piece of work that I think held up. The robust-allocation submission for the news manual (`Lava cake −27, Ashes −19, Obsidian −14, Thermalite +14, Pyroflex −13, Magma +8, Sulfur +5`) is what I'd point at as the most defensible single-submission decision of the contest. Not the highest result on the board, but a fun problem and I learned a lot about market-news analysis working through it.
- **(Mostly) working strategies in every round.** Every shipped strategy had positive backtest and positive (or at worst non-negative) live PnL on every round, on every day. Not the most efficient versions of those strategies in practice (that's what [lessons_learned.md](./lessons_learned.md) is about), but consistently positive expectancy across five very different product universes, solo, under finals pressure. That floor matters separately from how high the ceiling went, and I'm proud of the work I did to hold it. On a real market I think these strategies would be more practical than they were in the contest — and that's the kind of learning I hoped to get out of competing.
- **Directing AI, not deferring to it — overriding the auto-tuner where it mattered.** The two strongest single results in this writeup came from disagreeing with what the AI / auto-tuner / first-cut backtest was telling me to ship.
   1. **Round 2 chunking rejection.** `final_strat_aco_max80_chunk1.py` hit ~785k local 3-day backtest, 87% above my shipped recipe at ~420k. A "ship-the-highest-PnL-variant" auto-tuner would have promoted it. I rejected it on the third scan as a local-matching-engine artifact — 1-lot child-order chunking exploits a backtester quirk that doesn't exist on the real exchange — and shipped the conservative recipe. On live submission the rejected branch produced only **27–30k**. Call confirmed. The file lives in the repo as the canonical "do NOT ship" research artifact ([`code/traders/round2/`](./code/traders/round2/)).
   2. **Round 3 manual cluster-aware shift.** The textbook Bayesian optimum was `(751, 836)`, EV ≈ 84.33 per counterparty — the answer an AI gets cleanly from the rules alone. I modeled that ~55% of teams would paste exactly that, and at least one further "AI-cluster-aware" cohort would shift to `(775, 875)`. So I went two steps past at `(771, 861)`, capturing the teams that had run the same Bayesian logic I had and stopped there. **World #7 on this challenge.** Auto-tools converge to the textbook optimum; modeling where they'll converge and pricing past them is the human judgment layer.

   These aren't the only examples (the R3 ITM-strike P3R3 carry-over, the R3 IV-residual threshold conservatization, and several of the [lessons_learned.md §0e](./lessons_learned.md) calls fit the same shape), but they're the cleanest. I use AI heavily and I'm comfortable saying so — the judgment calls in this repo are mine. Where I deferred to the AI / local-backtest delta instead of first-principles judgment (see [lessons_learned.md §0d](./lessons_learned.md)), I regret it.
